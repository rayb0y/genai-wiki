Interviewer: Thanks for making time. This summer I'm researching how AI can support innovation teams, or
just teams in general, whatever team you've been part of, but specifically you've been part of innovation
teams, capstones. I want to understand how team dynamics and facilitation help, for example, if you've
seen good facilitation in a team, what that looks like. There are no right answers, I'm just trying to
understand your real experience. Any questions before we start?

Interviewee 2: I don't have any questions.

Interviewer: Could you tell me about the kind of innovation work or teams you've worked in, what kind of
projects?

Interviewee 2: Most innovation projects I've worked on have been at CMU, mostly from classes that had very
vague context. IPD was a little vague even though it had a structure, and the capstone was vague for us in
terms of the prompt and what we had to do. That was one of the few innovation-heavy projects. Other
classes had group work but not a lot of innovation, mainly because of team dynamics: a few people were a
lot more invested than others, and it was difficult to balance how much effort certain people wanted to
put in versus the actual work. I thought viscom (visual communication) would be innovation-heavy but it
turned into AI-slop generating. I got good outcomes from it, user journey maps, service blueprints,
background, but in terms of actually innovating or making something, that fell off and it ended up
feeling like there was no real use case. Apart from IPD I don't think I've had a class that was really
innovation-forward. UXR was a great class but not necessarily innovation-forward.

Interviewer: Apart from innovation, and before, what kind of projects and teams have you worked on?

Interviewee 2: From work experience: my first role was as a product designer at a manufacturing company,
so a lot of what I designed were internal tools, dashboards for order-management systems, internal
payment-approval tools, to reduce manual work time. The manufacturing industry runs on calling and
contacts, traditional formats, calling someone or texting them on WhatsApp to get information. We built
internal tools and dashboards that cut that time. After that I made a complete change and became a UX
designer at a gaming company, which was cool because I had no gaming experience. It was an existing game
and you're very restricted to the audience you have, the game was on a decline, practically on a heart
monitor, so we had to make sure everything was getting more player attention and that players spent more
money. Even though it was restricted, it helped me think out of the box: the narrower it got, the harder
it was to think of what to do, which pushed me to think of new features or ways to change the game. I was
a UX designer there for about a year and a half.

At CMU all the projects have been very different from each other, and so have the teams. I haven't been in
the same team twice, which is good because I can work with a lot of different people now. One thing I know
for a fact is that working in a professional company was easier for me, because when you're hired everyone
is on the same wavelength and you have a common goal. At CMU there's a mismatch between the shared goals
and the personal goals people have, which makes it harder to work in groups. For the most part it's been
good. I would have expected more solid outcomes from certain projects, IPD was supposed to be that, but it
doesn't seem directly related to what I want to do in the future, and it's hard to mold a project that's
completely unrelated into your story. UXR and tech strategy are two classes that stay in the back of my
head. UXR really pushed me to step out, do research, and interact with people, and because it was so
rigorous in a short period it pushed me to do a lot more than I would have. Tech strategy was very
case-study based and structured so well that I can still trace things back to it, if I see a pricing
strategy I recognize it almost immediately.

Interviewer: Let's talk about teams. Think about the best team you've ever worked on. What made it work,
a specific person or anything else?

Interviewee 2: At CMU the team dynamic I've appreciated the most was my capstone team. One thing that
helped was having a similar goal and understanding where each other stood at the beginning. All of us were
thrown into an area none of us knew about, and, I'll be open, none of us were interested in the project.
There was nothing about it that prompted us to work more than we had to. In the first few days we quickly
realized where we all stood: a baseline of "I don't really care about this project or this company, it's
unrelated to me," and everyone was on the same page. Establishing that, and how we'd go about it, really
helped us work together. We were mainly motivated by our grades, which isn't the best, but by the third
phase we actually got into the project and were invested, partly because there was suddenly so much to do,
so we spent a lot more time researching, calling, and understanding the problem space. Our teamwork came
mostly from that shared baseline understanding of where we stood. None of us thought this would go in our
portfolio. It was demotivating in the first two phases, but by the third phase we were invested and the
volume of work pushed us through.

Interviewer: How did you build that shared understanding at the beginning?

Interviewee 2: It was our first group meeting. We read about it, had our first meeting with the company,
and right after we had a casual conversation, "what do you think, where do you stand?", and everyone had
visible disappointment on their face, like, why do we have this, other companies had better names. On the
first few client calls, someone had quit the company, the point of contact left, so morale dropped
further and we were like, where have we been put, this makes no sense, and nothing's working out. That
shared disappointment in the first conversations established that we were all on the same page.

What else made the team work was really good communication and complementary roles: we had the [business
teammate] as the business person, the [engineering teammate] doing engineering, and the [research/design
teammate] and I doing research and a bit of design. We put ourselves into separate boxes almost
immediately by taking initiative, "I can do this, I can do that", and nobody fought over it because we
each had established strengths. There wasn't really miscommunication. There were disagreements, but they
were handled well. Even when I said "I don't think we're doing enough with this," someone would say
"listen, this isn't high stakes right now, we need to refocus," and we'd adjust. The balance of someone
wanting to do more and someone else managing expectations was really helpful, and those roles rotated. We
were also very appreciative of each other, because none of us were doing something we wanted, so every
time someone pushed a bit more we'd say "thank you so much, this is amazing." We hyped each other up a
lot, positive affirmation really helped. In our peer evaluations not a single one was bad for any of us,
it was just good, good, good. In the first round of feedback we all told the [engineering teammate] that
we wouldn't have understood any of the engineering concepts without them, they kept sketching on the
board how the generator works and breaking it down, patiently, up until the third phase. It takes a lot
of patience to keep repeating yourself, so we were all very mindful and grateful that everyone was doing
things they didn't have to that helped all of us.

Interviewer: What does it look like when a team actually gets stuck? How do people behave, what did it
feel like, was it recurring?

Interviewee 2: I'll focus on the capstone team since it was the longest. We got stuck on the same thing a
lot, couldn't move forward. It wasn't even that it was ambiguous, we just couldn't see a path forward,
which is frustrating, because we were doing all these things and had a solution, but we still had to stick
to the phases of the process even though at the end of phase 4 the solution we had was basically the same
as phase 1. It got frustrating to keep going back to the same thing over and over. When we were really
stuck we were all stuck together, none of us handled it too well, we were all super frustrated, "we don't
know what to do." We relied a lot on faculty support, but the unhelpful reviews and suggestions pushed us
to think "okay, we're not getting anywhere with this, but we still need to put a project out."

Interviewer: Let's talk about a few specific stuck moments.

Interviewee 2: The first big one: we had two solutions. We submitted the assignment and got reamed by the
faculty, everything they had approved the day before suddenly wasn't making sense to them, and we were
really confused, do they want us to change? That was one of the first all-nighters. We were stuck because
what we'd pitched wasn't working, one was so close to the finish line but still not working. Staying up,
we just brainstormed a lot, "maybe these are our potential ideas, they might not be realistic, but let's
put them down and generate what we have to." The thing I mentioned about each of us taking up parts fell
into place naturally, "I'll do this section," someone else "I'll do a storyboard for this real quick."
When we were super stuck, all we tried to do was flesh out as much as we could so something would stick.
The outcome wasn't great, it was a practice run and an absolute mess, and morale was down, but we stood up
for each other a lot. In one meeting the first thing the [faculty] said was "who's making all the
decisions here?", and we said "we're all deciding together." That was because the [engineering teammate]
and the [business teammate] were the only ones talking, mainly because none of us wanted to speak to the
faculty, so they took the lead. The [research/design teammate] jumped in and said "hey, just so you know,
we're really good friends, we're having these discussions, there's no one person making these decisions."
We all pitched in in different ways.

Interviewer: When you got stuck and decided to flesh it out, did someone intervene, what started it and
what made it work?

Interviewee 2: It started out of sheer panic, we felt like we were running out of time and needed more
than we had. None of us stopped each other from trying to come up with something new, we were just trying
our hardest to come up with some solution that made sense. We were also very focused on faculty approval,
because we were lost about where we were going and really wanted them on board. We didn't realize we
didn't actually need that approval as much, we could have just done what we wanted, but we were focused on
that for a long time. After our practice runs flopped, we had a whole intervention with the faculty in our
room, going through our first solution that they'd put comments on, and we said "you said x, y, z," and
they said "you're not doing this project for us, just consider, if you were to put something out, what
would it be, and what you already have is great." So a whole life's panic shifted into going back to our
original solution. It started from panic, we brought a lot of ideas, did a lot of things, got feedback
that we're not doing this for their approval, and reverted to everything we'd had from the beginning.

Interviewer: So the negative feedback, you focused a lot on it and then changed direction. How would you
deal with negative feedback if it wasn't from faculty, if it came from a random person in the cohort, or
an AI saying "these are the holes, these are the gaps I see," from somewhere that isn't a position of
power?

Interviewee 2: I think we'd be more open to constructive feedback. If you or anyone in the cohort said
"hey, maybe consider this," it's also the way it's phrased that matters a lot. We got a lot of
constructive feedback. What threw us off was that something had been approved and then the feedback was
completely different, that threw us way off. Throughout the process we got a lot of cohort feedback and we
were more open and appreciative of it because it gave us more ideas and didn't feel like a set direction,
which it does when it comes from faculty or a position of power. If faculty say "you should consider
this," we'd only consider that. If you or [a peer] said something, even three opinions, we'd think "oh,
this makes sense, we don't have to do the second part but we can focus on the first." It feels very
concrete when it comes from faculty, and more constructive when it's from peers or any other platform
assessing what I'm doing.

Interviewer: How did you keep track of your research, decisions, and reasoning, the memory of the team,
your shared knowledge?

Interviewee 2: We just had a common Google Doc, a Figma, and a WhatsApp chat. Any new information went on
the Google Doc and got discussed in a team meeting if it was new or relevant. We had set documents for
each phase, phase one doc with all the research, phase two doc, and so on, working on the same sheets, a
common Figma, common doc, and a WhatsApp chat.

Interviewer: If someone new joined your team in the middle, how would they get up to speed?

Interviewee 2: They would have a very difficult time.

Interviewer: And at work?

Interviewee 2: At work it was simple. We had shared documents, a Google-Doc-like internal company
platform. Since it was a gaming company we had a lot of design specs, art specs, product specs, dev tools,
everything, all placed in the same table format, and anyone new would go through them in order. First the
product spec: what the feature does, how it works, the aim, the product goals, design goals, what we want
revenue to look like after release, and the mechanics of the feature. Then the design spec: I'd have a
full user flow on a FigJam, a user persona, exactly what's being changed, and my reasoning. Then the
actual design document, all my Figma screens. Then the art document, all the art inspired by the Figma
docs. A new person would go through it depending on where they stand: a developer only needs the final
design and the product goals, an artist doesn't need the product context and works straight off the
design flows. It was consolidated really well in the order you'd read it. My handoff document had
everything structured: use cases, access flows, unique screens, edge cases, everything we'd have to
consider. Once art was approved, the final UX designs sat right next to the screens with all the dev
changes, one shared big document, so someone new could pick up almost immediately.

Interviewer: If your team disagreed, how did you resolve it?

Interviewee 2: We gave each other a little space, and there was never a moment where another person didn't
pitch in. As an example: when we were solutioning what the final product would look like, I had a vision
of going above and beyond, "maybe we should do this and this," and the [engineering teammate] said "this
project doesn't even matter, we don't care about it, they can do whatever." That was disappointing,
because I get it, I'm not invested either, but I'd want my outcome to be something nicer, I cared about
that even if she didn't. In those moments the discussion never went too far, we'd stop after disagreeing
and take a break from it, and the other members would mediate, validating what I was saying and what she
was saying and finding a middle ground. In this case the [research/design teammate] and the [business
teammate] said "we could do the first part of what you're saying, the final doesn't have to be so big,
maybe it's a small change, you don't have to focus on that many visuals, but we still have to make it look
good to a certain extent." It felt validating for both of us, and I said "I can work with that." There
needs to be an openness to change, which there was, so it was easily resolved. Stepping away, having
someone step in, and both of us being open to feedback really helped.

Interviewer: Were there disagreements that never got resolved?

Interviewee 2: No, because everything happened in the moment, it got sorted and we moved on.

Interviewer: Where do you think the real thinking happened, in the meetings, or other places, alone, or
two of you discussing and suddenly getting an idea?

Interviewee 2: Definitely in the in-person meetings. We did not have any real thinking in our online group
calls, because the aim of the calls was just to discuss and decide what needed to be sorted. Sitting in
person is when you could bounce ideas off each other. If I said something everyone would chime in, because
some people are generally introverted and it's hard to draw them out online, but sitting together you can
say "okay, what do you think, tell us," because you see their face and how they react. Much easier in
person.

Interviewer: Was everyone involved in those discussions, or were some people more influential in side
conversations rather than the formal ones?

Interviewee 2: I don't think we had anyone overly influential. When the faculty came, the [engineering
teammate] and the [business teammate] would speak because they had the most context, but they were
speaking for the group, saying what we'd all discussed. There weren't side conversations with two people
taking a lead separately, all the discussions happened together. That was partly because we did not think
about our capstone beyond our hours in the classroom, we didn't go home thinking about it or work on it at
home, everything happened within the classroom in that period.

Interviewer: Let's talk about before AI. How long did you work before AI was generally used, and what did
your work look like?

Interviewee 2: I started using AI at CMU, that was my first actual use. I wasn't using AI at work at all.
Maybe the product team used it to generate specs a little, but in design there was no AI usage. The first
time I used AI was at CMU and I personally don't like it because of how unstructured it is and the kind of
outcomes you get. Design work and AI work don't mesh well together for me. A creative process needs to
have, the whole point of artistic expressiveness is that it leads back to you and what you're doing,
that's the point of being designers or making art, you don't do it for profit, sure if it's profitable
that's great, but you do it out of your understanding of the things you like and what influences you. AI
influencing the artistic process is not something I appreciate. How much I dislike it increased in the
visual communication class because it was so forced, not every aspect of your design process has to use
AI. It really annoyed me that they wanted the research to come out of AI, why would our research be fully
AI, I don't understand that. Every outcome we were building toward was a vibe-coded app on [Figma Make],
and it was subpar at best. If we had taken that time and learned actual visual communication skills,
even one week on how good typography works, that would have made more sense for a class named visual
communication. You don't do art or design for it to look copy-pasted like everything else, everything
just looked the same. So I don't like it at all.

Interviewer: Do you use AI right now for anything?

Interviewee 2: I do, but never to actually design anything. I'm in an RA position on something completely
new that I don't know about, so I use [Claude] to find more sources for me, or to ask what something
means when I have an actual document, like "what is this kind of document, what does this mean, what's the
importance of this legal thing," that's when I go to [Claude]. I've been trying to use it as little as I
can and understand things from my end. One time I tried using [Claude] to synthesize research, I gave it
three documents and said "synthesize this," and the result was the most surface-level thing, everything
that matched across the documents, no nuance, it wasn't picking up on the things I wanted it to pick up
on, it felt redundant. So I left it, sat through it myself, it took an hour, but now I know those three
documents like the back of my hand and got the insights I actually wanted. Earlier at CMU I used AI for a
lot of copy-pasting, we had weekly readings and I didn't really do them because it felt like, since they
know we have AI, they give us more work. If you gave me one reading a week I'd actually read it and write
a small reflection for a class discussion, but if you want a student to do three things at once while
working on something else it's really difficult. Coming to CMU, the workload has increased compared to
cohorts two years ago, because they know we use AI and do things more quickly. But the point of a
master's program is that we develop better thinking skills for each class. Even the AI-and-IPD class, I
had so many expectations, and the AI aspect was just copy-pasting things into Google Colab, when he could
have taught us how smaller things work, what certain functions mean, what actual design systems look like.
So much potential wasted into "I can do this, let's make AI do it."

Interviewer: Is there anything AI has done that has genuinely changed how a team works for the better, or
how someone works?

Interviewee 2: The personal use case is the most helpful, it finds me sources I'd never have looked at or
realized, I wouldn't go through pages on Google. Finding specific information is really good. While
working, the same thing, finding specific sources to look into. I don't think I have anything else, not
that I've thought about.

Interviewer: If you could access an AI tool that helps a team not get stuck, or get unstuck, in the least
intrusive, least human-replacing way, can AI actually do that?

Interviewee 2: I feel like it can. A lot of it is doing a work analysis. Like the feedback that comes from
instructors versus from peers, the way it's formatted and said really matters. If an agent or chatbot is
analyzing the work we've done and the only thing it's saying is "have you considered xyz, what makes sense
and what doesn't," that helps. In our final presentation, [a peer] ran our full presentation through
[Claude] and asked "does this story structure make sense, does it work for telling a story," and we
thought "maybe this section can go up and we can introduce a video later." It helps you consolidate your
thoughts and it's great for reviewing. The way you ask questions matters. The input from different tools
was different, I was running [Claude] and someone else was running [Gemini] doing the same thing, and the
responses were very different, we took that into account too. Mainly it's about the tone, having
"what you can do better," and if it's just assessing your work it doesn't feel intrusive. You should be
able to opt in, "here's a tool if you want to assess what you've done so far and want feedback in the form
of what the faculty would say," and you can implement that. But the work before that should still happen
from us, it should be a more refining thing.

Interviewer: When in the process should no AI be in the room at all?

Interviewee 2: The ideation with AI was pretty good, we started from based ideas and expanded our
understanding. The big no-no is the initial stages of understanding what we actually want the problem to
be, because we tried and it gave us everything generic that wasn't working with what the faculty wanted.
The research and figuring out the scope of the project, big no to AI. But once you figure out what you're
actually doing, using it to ideate, analyze, or give you more suggestions works.

Interviewer: You've used innovation frameworks at CMU, design thinking, lean, and so on. How has AI
changed those frameworks, though I don't think you have experience with them before AI?

Interviewee 2: No, I don't, so nothing.

Interviewer: If you could design an AI tool for innovation teams, what would it do, you talked about
verification, summarizing, and giving ideas. What would make you trust something like that?

Interviewee 2: If the feedback and the pieces it recommended were received positively by a faculty. One
way to build confidence is, say we have a final presentation and want it analyzed to see if it works. If I
upload it, and I had faculty feedback plus changes suggested by the system, and once I made the changes
the faculty visibly approved the direction, I'd trust it more, because I'd know it's the right direction.
I like someone in authority to say "that makes sense," because I might not know everything and could just
pick up whatever is being said, and those changes work because they're validated by someone else. It's
difficult, and I don't know if I'm thinking this way because of how focused we were on what the faculty
wanted in the IPD project, but if something assessing our work gave us feedback that aligned with what the
faculty actually wanted, that would be positive and we'd keep using it. At work that onus would go to the
manager, there are always people in the loop assessing what you're doing. The first time I show my manager
something, she says "have you considered x, y, z," because she knows more than I do. Next time I've learned
to think of x and y, but maybe I don't know what z is, and if something else were helping me think of that,
the manager is still checking it, it's just confirmation that you're going in the right direction and
didn't miss anything. You learn from what you're doing anyway, but having that confirmation helps.

Interviewer: Final question: what should AI not do in a team?

Interviewee 2: This is unrelated to group work, but when I put up certain documents it paraphrases them,
even if I say make no changes. I was trying to transcribe an image and said "do not change what's written,
just transcribe image to text," and what I got was everything paraphrased from its own understanding. AI
can't do that, it literally cannot write it word for word, and I wouldn't have known that, so there should
be some awareness of AI's limitations. I didn't know, it felt like a waste of time, so I just googled an
image-to-text tool, put the image in, and it did exactly what I wanted.
