# The IPD Capstone process, as run

The phase-by-phase reality of the CMU III IPD Capstone, drawn from one team's full set of assignments, submissions, and teaching-team feedback across a semester. All sponsor, product, project, and individual identifiers are removed; what stays is the process and, more valuably, the patterns in how the faculty redirect teams. Those redirections are a corpus of real facilitation moves, which is exactly what the Socratic questioner in [[innovation-team-agent-architecture]] is meant to emulate. The framework is [[inpd]]; the methods are catalogued in [[innovation-process-methods]].

## Shape of the course

A team works one sponsor's brief across the four iNPD phases over a semester. Each phase has a run of small graded checkpoints (research plans, method work, canvases, criteria) and ends in a set of professional deliverables, usually a sponsor presentation, a short video, and a written brief or report. Sponsors meet the team bi-weekly and attend the phase presentations. Interview volume is explicit and high: at least 25 conversations in Phase 1, building to 75 to 100 across the semester including concept and prototype feedback. The course opens with a team contract before any project work (see [[team-dynamics]]).

## Phase 1: Identify, opportunities in context

The team scans the world with PESTLE (SET at a minimum), talks to at least 25 experts and stakeholders with focused questions, consolidates everything into a shared workspace (the faculty suggest NotebookLM as a research repository), brainstorms at least 25 opportunities, and down-selects to one or two to present. Opportunities are framed as problems and gaps, not solutions, "a list of problems you can fall in love with." The phase ends in an Early Insights and Opportunity presentation to peers and faculty, not sponsors, that builds an argument for one opportunity.

## Phase 2: Understand, what people value and need

The team writes a research plan (which methods, which stakeholders, which questions, when to synthesize), then runs people-research: interviews, observation (shadowing, fly-on-the-wall, walk-a-mile), guerrilla research, intercepts, and user-directed activities like journals and photos. Findings are organized with a Lean VOA (Value Opportunity Analysis) canvas to separate need-to-haves from nice-to-haves and to compare against the current state, and distilled into early product criteria stated as benefits, not features. Visual artifacts (2x2s, experience blueprints, affinity diagrams) make the thinking tangible. The phase delivers a sponsor presentation, a one-to-two-minute video, and a roughly ten-page brief report with an executive summary.

## Phase 3: Conceptualize, give form to ideas

Ideation is wide and deliberately not stopped at surface ideas. A validation plan identifies who will give feedback before there are concepts to show. A Build-A-Thon day makes 2D and 3D prototypes to find clarity and new ideas in the making. Concepts are refined with a strategy, not at random, and tested with target stakeholders. The team distills to a single concept it can pitch in two minutes on one slide, then delivers a Phase 3 sponsor presentation focused on the selected concept, what it is, how it might work, and how it meets the criteria, rather than final designs.

## Phase 4: Realize, depth, detail, and de-risking

The team refines with real users (catching up to the 75 to 100 target), moves the solution from a black box to a clear box where every component and function is specified, builds next-generation prototypes, and where relevant a technology roadmap. On the market side it sharpens customer archetypes (demographics and psychographics), sizes the market with a TAM/SAM/SOM, and builds the business model with a Lean Business Model Canvas. The phase, and the course, ends in a final report, presentation, and video.

## What the teaching team keeps redirecting toward

The single most useful thing in the record is the consistency of the feedback. The same handful of corrections recur across phases and teams, and each is a facilitation move the AI questioner would want to make. Stated as the redirection:

Solve the people problem, not the sponsor's business problem. A PESTLE that centered the sponsor and its revenue was judged incomplete: "focus more on the people problem. If you solve the people problem, the sponsor will have no difficulty solving the business problem." The repeated push is from the company outward to the humans and the trends that are not about the company at all.

Opportunities are not solutions, and do not prescribe before you have defined the problem. Summaries that ended in "what the sponsor should do" were called premature, because "you still need to figure out the opportunity and define the problem."

De-feature your criteria. The strongest recurring note: criteria written as features ("presets," "software enhancements") should be taken back a step to the value, benefit, or emotion behind them. "By focusing on benefits in the criteria the team can have more creative, broad-based conceptualization and a better solution. Features bias you toward a solution." The same applies to the VOA: capture the emotion and value, not the design requirement.

Take the team out of the storytelling. A report that read as "what we did and what we learned," foregrounding the team's journey and its tools, was redirected: "focus on the challenge, the problem, the users and their experience, not on your experience. Move beyond the tools and report the understanding." A method shown is not an insight communicated.

Outlines, not bullet lists. Bullets show the what; an outline forces the why and the who, which is the groundwork for clean storytelling.

Know the why, and make it explicit. The recurring gate question is whether the team knows the why behind the opportunity and the decision. Feedback repeatedly notes that depth and richness present in a team's talk went missing from its written record, and asks for the why to be surfaced.

Keep biases in check and do not validate a solution. Teams are told to avoid trying to confirm a pre-chosen answer, to revisit affinity diagrams for configurations they missed, and to define the current state they are comparing against so an outsider understands it.

Research questions are not interview questions. A methods list submitted without the thinking behind it was marked incomplete, with the reminder that a plan needs what you know and do not know, what you need to find out, and the questions you intend to ask, which are not the same as what you hope to learn (the same point as [[innovation-process-methods]] on design research).

In presentations, control the information flow and drop the jargon. Reveal dense slides with builds so the audience digests one point at a time, avoid leading with process or methods, avoid grandiose language and unsubstantiated claims, and onboard the audience through narrative.

## The faculty stance on AI in the process

The program is explicit and worth noting for the faculty interviews. AI is permitted only through CMU-approved, FERPA-compliant tools, NotebookLM is actively recommended as a Phase 1 research repository with a source-validation step, and prompting is called out as "even more crucial to get right" in the concept phase. But the stance is firmly that the work is "specific, contextual, and human," and the instruction is to "develop the critical communication skills that make you more valuable than a tool." This is the same scoping-and-verification tension, and the same human-voice concern, the interviews raised ([[interviewee-2-analysis]], [[interviewee-3-analysis]], [[what-genai-is-good-and-bad-at]]).

## Why this matters for the project

Three direct links. The phase structure and its gate criteria are the knowledge the iNPD detector reasons over ([[innovation-team-agent-architecture]], [[inpd]]). The recurring redirections above are a labeled set of good facilitation moves, the exact behavior the Socratic modes are trying to produce ([[genai-as-socratic-facilitator]]), and a far better guide to where teams actually get stuck than intuition. And the teaching team is the population the [[faculty-interview-protocol]] targets; this record is the context for those conversations, and the "research questions are not interview questions" caution applies to the protocol itself.
