# Interview Protocol (Short, 25 min): III Capstone Faculty

The trimmed run-sheet for III capstone faculty who can only give 20 to 30 minutes. It keeps the four areas only a faculty member can answer and drops everything the student interviews already cover. The full guide, with rationale and the complete question bank, is [[faculty-interview-protocol]].

The order is deliberately front-loaded: the must-ask questions come first, because there is no time to warm up and you want the things no student could give you in hand before the clock runs out. Still record with consent, anonymize by default, and follow a good thread even if it costs you a later question.

## Run-sheet

### 0. Frame and warm-up (1 to 2 min)

State the purpose plainly (how AI might support innovation teams, no right answers), confirm consent and recording, and ask one orienting question:

- How many capstone or iNPD teams have you advised or assessed, over how many years?

### 1. Seeing and assessing individual contribution (8 min): the core

- At the end of a project, how do you actually figure out who did what? What do you rely on?
- Every deliverable has the whole team's name on it. How do you see through that to individual contribution?
- Do you use peer evaluations, and do you trust them? When do they mislead you?
- How early can you tell who a free rider is, and what do you do? Have you seen the reverse, a quiet member who did a lot and got no credit?
- If you could see one thing about each member that you cannot today, what would it be?

### 2. Feedback and authority (6 min)

- When you give a team feedback at a review, what do you expect them to do with it: treat it as a requirement, or as one option to weigh?
- Students often take faculty feedback as binding and peer feedback as a suggestion. Does that match how you see your feedback landing? Is that what you intend?
- Have you ever approved a direction and then, at the next review, felt it no longer worked? What happened, on their side and yours?

### 3. Stuck patterns (5 min)

- What does a stuck team look like to you before they would even admit it? What are the tells?
- Where in the process do teams most reliably stall, and what usually gets them unstuck: you, a question, a deadline, or something on the team?

### 4. Reaction to the design (5 min): pitch only here

Describe it plainly: an AI that does not give answers but watches the team's logged work and, at the right moment, asks the team one question about a decision they have not justified; plus a private channel where a member can raise a concern that becomes a neutral team question; plus a contribution record of who did what.

- Would something like that help your teams, or get in the way of what you do?
- Would you trust its read of a team enough to act on it, and what would it have to show you first?
- What should it never do?

### 5. Close (1 min)

- What should I have asked? Who else, faculty or student, should I talk to?

## If you get even less time (12 to 15 min)

Ask only these four, in order:

1. How do you figure out who did what on a team, when every deliverable has everyone's name on it?
2. Have you ever approved a direction and then reversed it at the next review? Why?
3. What does a stuck team look like before they would admit it?
4. Would an AI that asks teams questions help you, or threaten what you do?

## What this version drops, and why

It cuts the sections that overlap the student interviews (what makes teams work, where real thinking happens, scoping) and the section resting on the weakest evidence (role types and balance, see [[proof-of-work-attribution]]). Those are worth asking when a faculty member gives you a full hour; they are not worth spending a scarce 25 minutes on. Filing the result follows the same process as the full guide: clean, anonymize, write a record and analysis, and compare the faculty view against the student view in [[interviews-synthesis]] rather than pooling them.
