# Interview Protocol: III Capstone Faculty

A semi-structured guide for interviewing Integrated Innovation Institute faculty who have advised or assessed capstone and iNPD teams. Faculty are a different population from the students in [[interviews-synthesis]] and are worth interviewing because they see many teams rather than one, they are the authority whose feedback the students described as binding, and they personally own the problem this project keeps circling: judging individual contribution on a team where every artifact carries everyone's name ([[proof-of-work-attribution]]).

Run it like the student interviews: roughly 45 to 60 minutes, recorded with consent, anonymized by default, and follow what is interesting rather than marching through every item. Save the design pitch (Section 9) for the end so it does not bias the earlier, descriptive answers.

## Versions and timing

This is the full guide, about 50 to 60 minutes if you probe properly. For faculty who can give less, use the 25-minute core in [[faculty-interview-protocol-short]], which keeps only the questions a student could not answer.

Rough per-section timing, and what to cut first if you run short:

- Section 1, background: 2 minutes. Keep it to a sentence.
- Section 2, what makes teams work: 5 minutes. Cut first, it overlaps the student data.
- Section 3, stuckness: 6 minutes. Must-ask.
- Section 4, feedback and authority: 7 minutes. Must-ask.
- Section 5, facilitation practice: 6 minutes.
- Section 6, individual contribution: 10 minutes. Must-ask, and the richest section.
- Section 7, roles and composition: 4 minutes. Cut early, the evidence is weakest here.
- Section 8, scoping: 4 minutes. Cut if needed, the student interviews cover it.
- Section 9, AI and the design reaction: 8 minutes. Must-ask, but pitch only at the end.
- Section 10, closing: 2 minutes.

The four must-ask sections (3, 4, 6, 9) are the faculty-unique core; protect them. This full guide keeps its narrative order, easy questions first and the sensitive ones last, to build rapport before the hard parts. The short version breaks that order on purpose and leads with the must-asks, because there is no time to warm up.

## What is new that only faculty can answer

The three student interviews each gave one team's view. Faculty open up questions a single student cannot:

- The base rates. Students each described their own stuck moments; faculty can say how common each pattern is across many teams, and which ones actually predict a bad outcome.
- The authority side of feedback. Students reported that faculty feedback lands as a binding direction while peer feedback feels like a suggestion ([[interviewee-2]]). Faculty can say whether they know this, what they intend, and why feedback sometimes reverses (the approved-then-rejected episode).
- How individual contribution is actually assessed. This is the practitioner reality behind the attribution research: what faculty do now, what they trust, and what they wish they could see.
- Whether an AI questioner threatens or helps their role, and what would make them endorse its output, which is the missing test of the trust-through-authority model ([[interviewee-2-analysis]]).

## Interviewing principles

- Ask for stories, not opinions: "tell me about a team that..." beats "do you think teams...". Get the specific case first, then the generalization.
- Stay non-leading. Do not name the project's hypotheses (stuckness, sycophancy, ownership) until Section 9; let faculty supply their own framing.
- Probe for the mechanism: when they say something worked or failed, ask what specifically made the difference.
- It is fine to disagree gently and ask them to elaborate; the student interviews were richest where the participant pushed back.

## Section 1: Background and vantage point

Aim: establish how wide a sample they are drawing on.

- Tell me about your role with capstone or iNPD teams. How many teams, over how many years, have you advised or assessed?
- What do you actually see of a team's work, and what stays invisible to you?

## Section 2: What separates teams that work from teams that do not

Aim: test the students' "alignment and shared purpose matter more than skill" finding against a larger sample.

- Think of the best capstone team you have seen and the worst. What separated them?
- How much of that is who is on the team versus how they work versus the project they were handed?
- Probe: when a team is struggling, do you find yourself wanting to change the people, the process, or the framing?

## Section 3: Stuckness from the outside

Aim: faculty pattern-recognition on how teams stall, and whether the iNPD structure helps or hurts.

- What does a stuck team look like to you, before they would even tell you they are stuck? What are the tells?
- Where in the process do teams most reliably get stuck? Is it the same phase each time?
- Some students describe being made to keep cycling through phases even when they have a settled answer. Do you see the phase structure helping teams, or sometimes forcing rework? (Probe gently; do not lead.)
- When a team gets unstuck, what usually does it? You, a question, a deadline, a reframing, or something on the team?

## Section 4: Feedback and authority

Aim: the authority side of the dynamic the students described. This is faculty-specific.

- Walk me through how you give a team feedback at a review. What are you trying to get them to do with it?
- When you suggest a direction, what do you expect them to do: treat it as a requirement, or as one option to weigh?
- Students sometimes experience faculty feedback as a binding instruction and peer feedback as a suggestion. Does that match how you see your feedback landing? Is that what you intend?
- Have you ever approved a direction and then, at the next review, felt it no longer worked? What happened on the team's side, and on yours?
- How do you tell a team their idea is weak without shutting down the discussion?

## Section 5: Facilitation practice

Aim: learn from expert facilitators, since this is what the AI questioner is modeling.

- How do you help a team without doing the thinking for them? Where is the line?
- Do you tend to ask questions or give answers when a team is lost? Can you give an example of a question that unlocked a team?
- When a team has interpersonal conflict, when do you step in and when do you let it run? (Probe: one student argued a mediator who calms conflict too early does harm.)
- How do you handle the team where one or two people dominate and others go quiet?

## Section 6: Seeing and assessing individual contribution

Aim: the proof-of-work problem from the person who has to solve it. Likely the richest section.

- At the end of a project, how do you actually figure out who did what? What do you rely on?
- Every deliverable tends to have the whole team's name on it. How do you see through that to individual contribution?
- Do you use peer evaluations? Do you trust them? When do they mislead you?
- How do you spot a free rider, and what do you do about it? How early can you tell?
- If you could see one thing about each member that you currently cannot, what would it be?
- Have you seen the opposite problem, a quiet member who contributed a lot but got no credit?

## Section 7: Team formation, roles, and composition

Aim: how faculty think about who is on a team and whether role gaps matter. Hold this loosely, since the evidence on role balance is mixed ([[proof-of-work-attribution]]).

- How are capstone teams formed, and how much say do you have in it?
- When you look at a team, do you think in terms of roles people play? Have you seen a team fail because a function was missing rather than because someone was weak?
- Do you see members knowing what their role is, or is role confusion itself a problem?

## Section 8: Scoping and the front end

Aim: Interviewee 3's headline request, the fuzzy front end.

- How do teams handle the early, open phase: scoping the problem and deciding how much to research?
- Do you see teams over-researching or under-researching? How do you help them find the right amount?

## Section 9: AI in team work, and a reaction to the design

Aim: current reality of AI in student work, then the stakeholder reaction to the project's design. Pitch only here, at the end.

First, the current reality:

- What have you seen AI change about how teams work, the quality of what they produce, and what they learn?
- What worries you about it? Have you seen it used badly, or used well?
- Has it changed how you assess work, or how much you can trust a deliverable as a signal of the team's understanding?

Then describe the design and get their reaction. Pitch it plainly: an AI that does not give answers, but watches the team's logged work and, at the right moment, asks the team one question to make them examine a decision they have not justified; plus a private channel where a member can raise a concern that gets turned into a neutral team question; plus a contribution record that shows who did what.

- Would something like that help your teams, or get in the way of what you do?
- Would you trust its read of a team enough to act on it? What would it have to show you first?
- Is there a version of this that makes your job easier, for example by preparing teams for your reviews?
- What should it never do?

## Section 10: Closing

- What did I not ask that I should have?
- Who else, faculty or student, should I talk to about this?

## Open questions this protocol is meant to resolve

These are the project's current unknowns that faculty are positioned to close. They are starting targets, to revise after the first faculty interview.

- Base rates: how common is each stuck pattern, and which ones actually predict a poor outcome rather than just feeling bad in the moment?
- Why does authority feedback reverse, and do faculty know it is taken as binding? This is the mechanism behind the most destabilizing student stuck-moment.
- How is individual contribution assessed in practice, and where does that process fail? This is the empirical reality the [[proof-of-work-attribution]] research needs.
- Does expert facilitation actually work by asking questions, or by something the Socratic design is not capturing?
- Would faculty endorse the tool's output, which is the precondition for the trust-through-authority model from [[interviewee-2]] to function?
- Do faculty want an audience-rehearsal tool that preps teams for their reviews ([[interviewee-3-analysis]])?
- Is role clarity (members knowing their role) a bigger lever than role coverage (the team having the right mix), as the evidence in [[proof-of-work-attribution]] suggests?

## Filing the result

Each completed faculty interview is filed like the others: clean the transcript, anonymize, write a record page from [[interview-template]] and a deeper analysis, then update [[interviews-synthesis]] and append to the log. Faculty are a distinct population, so the synthesis should compare the faculty view against the student view rather than pooling them.
