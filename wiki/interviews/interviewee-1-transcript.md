**Interviewer:** Awesome. Let's begin. Can you tell me a bit about the kind of innovation work you've been involved in? For example, what kinds of teams, what kinds of projects, was it early-stage and exploratory or more defined?

**Interviewee 1:** I've had a mix of projects, though not so much a mix of teams; they're generally the same buckets. There'll be people doing design, visual or UX. There'll be people doing research. In my job I had a managerial position overseeing things and providing direction. And then there was the client side, who had some questions and some clarifications, almost like a resource. The projects ranged from quite open-ended, where the client just had a goal and didn't know how to achieve it, so my leads would come up with a research plan, and we'd use that research to structure the rest of the project. I wasn't part of the research team; I'd take the data from research, structure it into insights, and use it. I'd sit in on some research but not conduct it.

Some projects are very defined. They have particular flows or goals, or the technology they're capable of creating can do certain things and they just need the design and user experience to match what the technology can do. Those were typically rigid projects, not a lot of leeway for innovation, because they already had a type of technology and didn't want their customers to have too much of a mental-model mismatch. Sometimes you'd have to push on that and do research validation to prove that their original thing isn't very helpful in terms of mental models, and there are better ones to follow. Not every change is a crazy new change to your customer; sometimes the world has evolved around your product and you've not caught up yet. That was particular to some IT infrastructure products we had, B2B, very tight pipeline. They didn't like to deviate, and you'd have to prove that you can do validation and that their customers can adapt. In those more rigid projects the client's team would be more involved, as a resource to help us understand the technology or why it needs to be so rigid, because they were the experts and we were an agency. As an agency you can do your initial research, but you can't have the same depth as a nine-year-experience in-house product person, so we'd take a lot of insight from them and treat them as experts for the rigid projects.

**Interviewer:** Maybe unrelated, but having to validate your research when it's more rigid and trying to make changes, what was that process like? Was it taxing?
