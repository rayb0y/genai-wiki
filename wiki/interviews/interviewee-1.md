## One-line summary

A design professional's account of what makes innovation teams click or stall (shared goals and mutual respect, not skill), where her real thinking happens (out loud, with a sounding board), and a sharp first-hand read on where AI helps in innovation work (absorbing documents, confirming grounded facts) versus where it hurts (unverified "shaky foundations," sycophantic validation, and aggregating popular ideas rather than producing genuinely new ones).

## Key takeaways

- Teams click through give-and-take and mutual respect for each person's contribution, plus a common goal to rally around. The goal can even be an external adversary ("a common villain"), such as winning over a difficult client or winning a hackathon.
- The clearest cause of a stuck or bad team was the absence of any unifying goal: members had different objectives, no shared excitement about the topic, and no idea everyone was eager about. She would change the people, not the process.
- Her best thinking happens out loud with a sounding board that restructures her thinking and pulls her out of going in circles. The opposite failure is overthinking, which she breaks by "just starting somewhere."
- Selling innovation and validation to clients is mostly a money and persuasion problem. Her tactic: flesh out the preferred concept more than the alternatives, and pre-answer each stakeholder's likely questions (lead = granular how, manager = why it is a good choice, client = business case).
- Knowledge was held in a "truth document" (a shared drive of confirmed decisions, timeline, objectives, hierarchy, and links to concept files) plus monthly designer knowledge-sharing workshops. Pain points: technical docs too dense for juniors, access and permissions, and messy research files where the "final clarified" version is hard to find.
- AI today helps most by absorbing large or technical documents fast. It hurts when people use it without verifying, building on a "shaky foundation" that crumbles when the AI fudged numbers or pulled the wrong thing.
- AI should be a grounded quick-resource (early onboarding, confirming a source from your own documents). It should not be used for validation, because AI skews toward praise and "that's not the point of validation."
- For her to trust an AI tool it must be transparent, handle version and context correctly, not hallucinate, and not flatter. The burden of catching flattery currently falls on the user's self-awareness, which less AI-savvy people lack.
- Going to AI to generate ideas when stuck is "a flawed approach," because innovation means something new and AI aggregates the popular. Her test case: would AI have invented Spotify Wrapped, a cross-domain leap into music?

## Themes

- Stuck teams and what unblocks them: stuckness tracked to misaligned goals and missing shared purpose more than to skill.
- Thinking out loud / the sounding board: the value is a partner that restructures thinking, not one that hands over answers. Central to the AI-as-questioner thread.
- AI as aggregator, not innovator: her Spotify Wrapped argument is a first-hand version of the high-level-adaptor thesis. See [[genai-on-the-adaption-innovation-spectrum]] and [[what-genai-is-good-and-bad-at]].
- Verification and "shaky foundations": unverified AI output others build on is the failure mode she has lived. See [[what-genai-is-good-and-bad-at]] and [[rag-explained]].
- Grounding and the "truth document": retrieval over your own documents as the safe use of AI, which is the [[rag-explained]] and [[getting-the-most-out-of-llms]] pattern.
- Sycophancy and trust: the "praise response" maps to the RLHF caveat in [[how-genai-works]].
- Facilitation and frameworks: a good facilitator lowers direction-anxiety; she finds AI useful when it asks two or three follow-up questions she can map to a known framework.
