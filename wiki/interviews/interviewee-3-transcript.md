**Interviewer:** I've explained what the project is about. There are no right answers, I'm just trying to understand your real experience. Any questions before we start? Can you tell me about the innovation work you've been involved in, what kinds of teams and projects?

**Interviewee 3:** I'll start with the very first experience at CMU, the invisible-technology project, which I still feel was very innovative and very loose. That was one of the first innovative experiences because it was a concept that even people doing PhDs are currently pursuing, so it was genuinely a good idea, even if we didn't approach it the right way. After that, in terms of innovation, the next was the capstone, proper innovation. We had other teams, engineering, business, project teams, but those weren't really big on the innovation side. The capstone and the invisible-technology project are the two innovation ones. The third would be a self-directed project I did out of my comfort zone, not exactly innovative but not usually pursued, a good idea I want to carry forward later this summer.

**Interviewer:** What formed the teams, who was in them?

**Interviewee 3:** The invisible-technology project was [the interviewer], me, and [a teammate], a concept of invisible technology. That was a strong team because we had different perspectives, interests, and backgrounds, which came together to handle each section of work. That's why it was one of the more progressive teams, even with all our differences. The capstone team we didn't have a choice in, we were just given the team and had to work with it. That wasn't even a choice, just a situation to deal with. The invisible-technology project gave us more of a choice about who was really helping, so it was more flexible than the capstone.

**Interviewer:** You said the invisible-technology project wasn't done the right way. Elaborate?

**Interviewee 3:** We approached it as a product first, which was too much of a step up, too ahead of its time. Going straight into business thinking, treating it as a product, was something it wasn't ready for. It needed more research before product thinking. We all started with product thinking because we were doing IPD and courses that put us in that mindset. The real thing should have been to start with research, take it as a research process, and then develop it into a product. That's why it didn't fail exactly, but it got slow. It also didn't align with a lot of teammates who had the idea we'd build it as a product eventually, so it slowed down.

**Interviewer:** Think about an innovation team that actually made it work.

**Interviewee 3:** The capstone. One reason was it was kind of a private project. Another was that the team was everyone I hadn't worked with except one person, so it was a new opportunity to collaborate. In the first few meetings we set ourselves up to not just focus on the grade but on getting the project to be the best, and to challenge ourselves we decided we'd make it a patent-level project so the sponsoring company would pay attention. That goal-setting is one reason we pulled through the ups and downs, and there were a ton, we were very confused even in the middle. And of course it was graded, so we were already in the mindset that we had to get it done.

**Interviewer:** Was there a specific person or role that helped the goal-setting or made it work?

**Interviewee 3:** One person definitely helped keep us on track, they had the nature of getting things done, quickly completing work and not delaying. That's a good thing, but it's also sometimes problematic because it makes you cut time on things that actually need time. Creative thinking needs time to think about new ideas and perspectives, and you can't cut time there even when you don't have much, you cut it somewhere else. So sometimes it became a problem, but other times it really helped keep us on track.

**Interviewer:** What does it look like when your teams get stuck? A specific moment.

**Interviewee 3:** Getting stuck meant we didn't know exactly what to put down as our immediate next goal. We knew the big goal, but not what to do in the next couple of days. In the capstone, one of our ideas was quickly rejected by the faculty, almost a month's work, and once your idea is rejected you're blank, you have this canvas and it's just blank, you have to erase all that work and scrap the whole thought process. Something similar happened on the self-directed project when we were stuck on how to start, the project was so big, how do you start small and pick a starting point. We were just confused, debating amongst ourselves. Getting stuck, in both cases, is either everyone going blank and nobody discussing, or everyone discussing too much of things that make no sense, a chaotic discussion leading nowhere.

**Interviewer:** Was getting stuck recurring in the capstone?

**Interviewee 3:** It wasn't constant but it repeated a couple of times. First when the big idea we'd worked on got rejected. Second when the big idea was accepted and everyone was happy, but then choosing the details became problematic, what to propose versus keep for future improvements. There was one part about the mechanics of the idea where a teammate and I debated continuously, balancing what's proposable and easy to convey to the audience versus what's easily doable, balancing practicality and perceived ability. We debated endlessly and my teammate wasn't providing enough supporting points, which became a problem, but we tried to sort it out.

**Interviewer:** What actually helped when you got stuck?

**Interviewee 3:** Discussing, because going blank means you're doing nothing. Discussion is good as long as everyone is respectful, though at one point we went off track and had heated conversations. Discussion gets most of the thoughts out, even the rejected ones, and somewhere in that chaos one idea or one modification clicks. For our capstone we proposed an invisible transportation system for the city, a blue-sky idea that got rejected at the national discussions, but eventually they realized that's exactly what would solve the problem, moving the city's economical and logistical burden off the ground. We didn't treat it as a stupid blue-sky project, we thought it through as an actual project, got into the details and discussed it, and the discussions made us realize it was something we could propose.

**Interviewer:** What starts the discussions, the click moment, a question or a reframing of the problem?

**Interviewee 3:** The discussion always starts at the conflict of opinion. If there's no conflict of opinion you can never explore different elements. As long as we have different opinions we'll always have discussions, and conflicts get more results out of a discussion than everyone agreeing on the same idea. If everyone agrees, that's the one idea you work on, but if everyone has conflicting ideas you get different perspectives on the same project. So conflicts are really good. The only caveat is the possibility of getting into heated, disrespectful discussions, and that's about maturity, you need those conflicted discussions but in a respectful way.

**Interviewer:** What do you think about being easy to work with, is it work styles or something else?

**Interviewee 3:** It has different definitions for different people. For me, being easy to work with is someone ready to speak as well as listen to everybody's opinion. I'd love to listen to everyone's ideas but I also want the chance for everyone to hear mine. As long as that's happening, anyone is easy to work with. There are peculiar habits that annoy you in the moment, but in the long run they don't really affect you. As long as I'm being heard and everybody can speak up and give valuable inputs, that's what I'd define as easy to work with.

**Interviewer:** How did your team keep track of everything, research, decisions, reasoning?

**Interviewee 3:** For each phase we had a shared Google Doc, and we made tabs and labeled our work, the research, thoughts, and so on. For the interviews we did, we labeled each tab as the interviewer and the interviewee. Custom documents went in the drive. We had a shared notebook too, and [Canva] was the biggest tool for the presentation slides. The other thing was the project-room whiteboard, where we'd draw, write, or diagram things during discussions so we all understood.

**Interviewer:** Were there moments where people had different understandings of the same thing?

**Interviewee 3:** Yes, a lot. When we were discussing our pilot city we started with New York City, but different people had different opinions, some saw congestion as the main thing, others the multi-level housing, others curbside management. It was hard to gauge what to show, what to say when someone asks why New York City. The second was whether we were solving for effort, time, or convenience. Two teammates said time and effort, others said time and convenience, I was the only one who said effort and convenience. Three words, and we had almost a three or four hour discussion over it.

**Interviewer:** How did it get solved?

**Interviewee 3:** It didn't get solved entirely. We solved it by thinking about what the pilot would tell, what to focus on first and what comes next. If the pilot focuses on effort and convenience, then time could be a phase-two deliverable. We asked, if we launched the pilot today, what would be the first two things delivered, and that ended the four-hour discussion. We mentioned in the slides that we'd deliver these two first and then the third, basically timing it out, which comes first chronologically, then phasing the deliverables.

**Interviewer:** Who came up with that, one person or the group?

**Interviewee 3:** One person, [a teammate], who said, let's first think about what to get done in phase one, not the whole final idea, but what we can actually deliver in the very first phase. He came up with it and we started discussing the first two things we could propose meaningfully, and we all agreed and went ahead.

**Interviewer:** How did you resolve disagreements?

**Interviewee 3:** Just discussions, but whenever it got heated I'd take a step back. In a heated conversation you never achieve anything meaningful because both sides are fired up and not ready to listen. So I take a step back before something severe happens. I did that with a teammate, we had a heated discussion over which mechanism to use, they proposed one and I proposed another. I kept giving the point that my mechanism has these supporting points, what does yours have, and she wasn't ready to give the supporting points, which riled me up and heated it. So I pulled back, said let's pause and cool down, and when we resumed I explained why I was concerned and not convinced, and said if that's what we want to go with we can discuss it and go ahead. That's when both sides realized the two perspectives and she resolved it.

**Interviewer:** Where does the real thinking happen in a team, in meetings, in side conversations, or alone?

**Interviewee 3:** A bit of both, but more in discussions. You can't discuss without your research done, so the research and study beforehand really helps. But the real thinking-through comes via discussions, because when you say something, someone might have an opposing opinion, and that opposition is the stress-testing of the idea. You might be thinking about an idea in one way and not stress-testing it because you like it, so you need someone else to stress-test it before you commit. That's also how I work on individual projects, currently I'm doing one solo, and whenever I have an idea I throw it to an AI platform and tell it to stress-test it, because I'm attached to it, so it's better to have something else try to make it fail.

**Interviewer:** What about AI sycophancy, where it says "that's a great idea"? How do you deal with it?

**Interviewee 3:** I don't like that. I just tell it to prove it and critically test it, you don't have to support the idea if there's no reason to. I scoured the internet for a prompt to make it very critical and brutal. I tell it, here's the idea, stress-test it, critically analyze it, don't give me the sycophancy of "this is a good idea." I tell it to talk in numbers, what percentage deals with normal cases, what with edge cases, how reliable it is in different edge cases, and report the gaps where my idea fails. I have a few things I was working on, an autonomous-racing project about path planning, I wrote the idea, put the picture in, and told it to stress-test it and tell me the gaps.

**Interviewer:** How does AI show up in innovation work, your own or others'?

**Interviewee 3:** In my own work, no matter how much context you give AI, it still lacks the context you have because you're in the situation. In the racing project, I told [Claude] this is for a racing situation, and it still gave me compute-heavy ideas that took more computation time, not optimized for racing. It didn't have the on-ground reality, the tracks, how my car is. And since it doesn't have that context, it gives you an idea that's already been proposed, because it works on the internet model, the data available online. When you're trying to come up with something nobody has, AI fails, because it works on what's already been done. In that scenario you talk to it differently, you don't tell it to stress-test based on existing ideas, you tell it to stress-test based on the mathematics or logic and come up with its own logic, tell me whether this could work theoretically, not whether it exists. That's where AI limits innovation.

**Interviewer:** An example of AI being used badly in your teams?

**Interviewee 3:** In the capstone there were times people pawned off work to AI, getting everything done with AI and calling it a day. For tedious, laborious jobs like writing reports it's fair enough, it saves time. But for actual ideation or creative work it should be used less. You can start with AI, get a blueprint, but then edit it and check whether it fits, stress-test it, give it a visual check. Example, the final report for the capstone, a teammate had to write the design section and pawned it off to AI, and it had a lot of discrepancies, it had em-dashes, points we'd never included in the proposal, flat descriptions, and it didn't describe the core idea properly because it lacked context. They didn't check it and put it in the report, so another team member had to go through it, check it, and re-edit it to make it reportable.

**Interviewer:** Where is AI essentially useful in a project?

**Interviewee 3:** Mostly the laborious work. As long as the report is just being drafted, you give it the material and it puts it in a formal way, that's fair enough. Anywhere you're not meaningfully adding progress, just reporting or basic drafting. I used AI to pull research out, draft a pointwise resource for reference, draft formal emails, and for basic diagramming and imaging, if you want to convey an idea with a diagram you tell AI to make it, it saves time. All the laborious work can be shortened with AI, especially when you need many iterations under a time limit, the capstone was four months of iterating. But for actual design thinking you need to sit down and think through each idea and different perspectives, that's when you give inputs rather than take them.

**Interviewer:** If there was an AI in the room that keeps asking the team questions about the decisions they made, how would that go?

**Interviewee 3:** As long as it limits itself to the scope of the project, it would be helpful. For us it was a pilot project, so if the AI scopes to the pilot and asks questions around it, that prepares us for the audience, because the audience would ask similar questions. It makes us think our idea through, especially in domains we're new to. I'm doing a project with clinical retirees, an area I'm very new to, so letting AI ask questions about my idea helps me think it through from all perspectives, clinical, product, customer, regulation. That kind of AI would help a lot.

**Interviewer:** Follow-up. Imagine a Collaboration Agent designed just for you. You can complain to it, even rudely. It removes the emotion, logs it, and triggers a Socratic question to the team, so the team has to face and answer the question. Would that help?

**Interviewee 3:** Let me make sure I get it. So instead of a cluster of agents, I have my own agent, I give it all my complaints, it pulls that review, removes the emotion, takes the crux of the problem, and asks questions around that crux in a discussion meeting. In a way it could be helpful, but I think it takes the team spirit away. The reason is that's what I should be doing. If I have a complaint with a teammate, I should have enough maturity to take the emotion out and talk it through with them respectfully, at least let them know what's bothering me and ask what bothers them about me. That's what I should be doing as part of the team. The AI prompting the question would instigate a discussion we might be avoiding, but at the end of the day, doing it ourselves is what keeps the team spirit. It gets the question through and the discussion started, but it doesn't build the team spirit you need. In a short project like the capstone it can be helpful, but in the long run it lets you take the accountability away and give it to the AI, you're running away from the accountability of owning up to your problem rather than letting AI speak it out loud. You might think it resolves the problem, but in the long term it keeps everybody separated as well as connected. What actually makes a team is being able to talk through the problems that bother you. If you give that to AI, you kill the team spirit. Technically it's helpful, but in the long run it kills teams.

**Interviewer:** So you think ownership is really important?

**Interviewee 3:** It's important. You can be wrong, in the invisible-technology project we were wrong on so many points, each of us thinking about the problem differently. You can think about the direction differently, but owning it and talking it out in the team is important so you at least know what each person is thinking. If you give that job to AI, none of you know what each other is thinking, you're just discussing and resolving, like a board meeting once a week. Nobody knows how each person thinks about the project individually, which is important. I should know whether you're on the same page as me, because then I know what to discuss and how. If you're not on the same page I'd resolve that first and find a middle ground. If we think the same way, I can discuss different opportunities knowing you'd think similarly. It's more collaborative. Giving that to AI kills the team spirit in the middle ground.

**Interviewer:** Have you faced different levels of engagement, people not taking ownership?

**Interviewee 3:** Yes, in the capstone. There was a teammate mediating a heated discussion, just saying "let it go, let it go," which isn't the right way. Taking ownership and talking it out matters, whether you're right or wrong or on the same page, it's important to know how the other person is thinking. If I have the concern that an idea doesn't have enough supporting points, I should know why the other person still supports it, there has to be a reason. So at least I know the reason we're going with an idea even if it seems bad. The way I resolve it is to hear everybody, let everybody talk first, and then reframe my thinking so everyone realizes how I think. I hate when someone tries to mediate in a way that lets the discussion go or lets someone have their idea just for the sake of calming someone down. That's wrong, if my idea is bad, just tell me why.

**Interviewer:** How did you deal with that mediator?

**Interviewee 3:** I said it to their face, in the meeting. I told them, I realize you're trying to mediate, which is fair, I respect that, but this discussion needs to happen, heated or not, because it's stopping progress. Sometimes mediation is helpful, it's not entirely bad, but if it starts to hinder progress it's not good. If there's going to be a heated conversation, let it happen, what's the worst that can go wrong? In a professional environment everyone knows nobody is trying to hate on each other. If it gets too heated we can apologize later, that's not a problem, but at least we get through the problem. I did apologize to the person I had the heated conversation with afterwards, because we'd resolved the problem. If it had been mediated, we'd never have resolved it properly.

**Interviewer:** So arguments are necessary?

**Interviewee 3:** Yes, respectful arguments. If it gets too heated you take a step back and pause, then resume. But arguments are important, at least letting the person know why you don't agree with their idea, and hearing why they don't agree with yours.

**Interviewer:** Pointed question. The complain-to-agent, the peer-review thing that notes it down. Do you think there should be other factors the Orchestrator considers before bringing it up, not directly shooting a question, but checking whether it's in line with where the project is going?

**Interviewee 3:** One thing such a system could do, once it has taken in all the peer reviews, is report two things, the commonalities and the differences. What are the points everyone agrees on and why, and what are the points where members disagree. That sparks a discussion and lets everyone know, these are the points we agree on, now let's tackle these disagreements and find the core cause. When I complain I'd be speaking everything out, including things that aren't important, and the AI can give me the output that these are the points of disagreement and these are the points of agreement. Once the team knows exactly what it disagrees on, that's when you can frame the problem properly and start discussing it. That would be a helpful tool.

**Interviewer:** Let's talk about innovation frameworks, design thinking, IPD. How do they translate into team practice?

**Interviewee 3:** They help in the initial phase, design thinking, double diamond, but it's more about the project, every method applies to its own project. For us it was so vague that the normal IPD methods weren't even helpful. So it depends on the project. Different people think differently and choose different tools, that's fine, but it's more important to understand what your project is and what tools are most beneficial. It has nothing to do with the team, it's more dependent on the project.

**Interviewer:** How well does AI mix with these frameworks?

**Interviewee 3:** That's a big gap. Even in desk research, you try to think in your own scope but AI expands that scope too much and you get riled up in the details. You don't need too much detail, just enough to know the domain and work around it. You don't need to be a doctor to know about diabetes, but you need to know enough to build a product around it. AI doesn't know the scope of the project, and to scope it out is difficult because you don't know the domain well either, so you don't know how much to scope. That's where AI has the discrepancy, you get too much information or you miss the critical information. The starting phase is very fuzzy. I tried to make a skill file for [Claude] to scope it out, and that also sometimes fails.

**Interviewer:** If you could design an AI tool for innovation teams, what would it do?

**Interviewee 3:** First and foremost, a scoping mechanism. Based on the problem statement, scope the research resources, not too much, not too little. Have a mechanism that lets the team quickly get to know the project, and then they start problem-solving and design thinking. They don't need to be experts in the domain, just know enough to get around the problem. Even with IPD methods we don't have a mechanism to scope research, sometimes we steer away from the problem, sometimes we get too into it. If you can scope your research, that really helps, knowing exactly how much you need. You can expand research endlessly and get nothing, or get exactly what you need with the right papers. It should be worth the time you give to research.

**Interviewer:** What would make you trust something like that? What would it need to demonstrate?

**Interviewee 3:** It should let me know what the domain is about and the current situation, and where my problem statement stands within it. For the diabetes-management system, it should tell me where my problem statement rests, what area, and give me enough articles or research papers to read to know the domain. In terms of deliverables, a list of resources that would be helpful, but not too clinical, not too domain-specific, laying out the domain in layman terms as if I'm a new person researching it. It's tough to describe what you want, it's an opposing thing, you want to know enough but don't know exactly what enough is. That's the problem to solve, what framework or method tells you what enough is, then tell the AI to scope the project to give you enough.

**Interviewer:** Let me reframe. What would make you stop using it?

**Interviewee 3:** If I'm spending too much time. This happened on the current project, I spent too much time trying to know about diabetes and the medical process, and I realized I was getting too clinical, into the biology of it, which isn't required, my consumers aren't medical professionals, they're families dealing with diabetes. So I stopped reading those resources when it got too detailed and out of my scope. My job is to work around the technology of the product, so when I come across a resource too deep into the user side, I limit myself, because someone else develops that side. I put a stop-loss whenever I'm straying from my field of research. I focus on what I need to deliver. When I'm getting too much detail into a section that isn't required, I stop, tell it to give me a brief summary, read that, and move on.

**Interviewer:** What are the things AI should not do, that would feel like a violation, besides eating into team dynamics?

**Interviewee 3:** It should not come up with any kind of proposals or verdicts. Sometimes AI gives you basic ideas and then a verdict, "my verdict could be this." I don't want that, because the verdict might or might not be correct, it's working around its own memory. This happens a lot in my technical projects, it gives a verdict like "this is how you can work around it, this is the equation," and I think sure, but what's the caveat? I don't know the caveat, I don't know if it's what I want to work with or has problems I'd face. The AI doesn't have the full context, and when it gives a verdict it's telling me this is the better option, which I don't want. I don't want its opinion about which idea is better, I want to make that decision myself. It's like asking interview questions, we're told not to be biased so we don't lead the interview. I want AI to give me the plain pros and cons and let me decide based on my context. Even if I make the wrong choice, I'll at least know why I made it, instead of going with it because AI said so.

**Interviewer:** Final question. Anyone in or outside our cohort who'd be a good person to talk to about this?

**Interviewee 3:** Talk to [a peer], he uses AI a lot and is doing a startup, so he'd have experiences in the context of startups, what was helpful or problematic. With startups, innovation is a big thing, they need to think big to get through the bootstrapping phase.

**Interviewer:** You talked about ownership. How do you think you build ownership in a project?

**Interviewee 3:** You build ownership by bringing different perspectives into the team. If you do nothing, you're in the project just for namesake. You build ownership by actually having parts of the project you've done, so you really feel you did this project by contributing meaningfully, and that only comes when you discuss, get ideas out, research them. AI helps in communication, but in the long run you'd never know exactly what your team is doing, so you'd never know how significantly you can contribute. To contribute meaningfully you need to know where the team is going and the exact problem space it's targeting and where it's lacking so you can help. So it's important to be on the same page, and that only happens with discussions, even arguments.

**Interviewer:** How do you bring in people who are just not interested?

**Interviewee 3:** Eventually you accept the situation, but the best thing I try is to pinpoint them in a meeting, ask them explicitly, "what's your opinion?" This happened with a teammate in the capstone who wasn't talking much, so I'd explicitly ask her to express her opinions. That gives an opportunity for everyone to listen. But if they're absolutely disinterested and not ready to speak, they end up becoming invisible, and that loses them ownership of the project. You might say it's a project you were in, but you did nothing in it, so it's an empty claim, it's not really yours. That bugs me a lot. If I'm in a project I need to significantly contribute and know how I filled the gaps.
