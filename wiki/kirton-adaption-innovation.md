# Kirton's Adaption-Innovation (A-I) Theory

A-I is a theory of cognitive style, meaning how people prefer to solve problems and be creative. It was developed by the cognitive psychologist Michael Kirton ([Kirton 1976, *Adaptors and Innovators: A Description and Measure*, J. Applied Psychology](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ148010); expanded in [Kirton 2003](https://www.regent.edu/journal/emerging-leadership-journeys/kirton-adaption-innovation/)). It is the foundation this wiki maps GenAI onto in [[genai-on-the-adaption-innovation-spectrum]].

## The continuum: doing things better vs doing things differently

Everyone sits somewhere on a single continuum from high adaption to high innovation ([businessballs summary](https://www.businessballs.com/change-management/kirtons-adaptation-innovation-theory/)).

Adaptors prefer to do things better. They improve, refine, and optimize within the existing paradigm, meaning the accepted theories, rules, methods, and frames of an organization. Kirton describes the adaptive style as active, creative paradigm maintenance.

Innovators prefer to do things differently. They reframe the problem and break or replace the paradigm. Kirton describes the innovative style as active, creative paradigm shift.

Both ends are creative; they are two routes to creativity, not more creative versus less creative ([Regent review](https://www.regent.edu/journal/emerging-leadership-journeys/kirton-adaption-innovation/)). Position is also relative. A person is more adaptive or more innovative only compared with others, and someone can be the innovator in one group and the adaptor in another.

## Style is not the same as level

This is the point most often missed, and the one that matters most for the GenAI mapping.

Cognitive level is capacity: intelligence, competence, knowledge, the more-or-less of performance. Cognitive style is manner: the preferred approach to solving problems. The two are independent. A highly capable person can be adaptive or innovative, and style says nothing about how good the solutions are ([KAI Foundation](https://kai.foundation/adaption-innovation-measure-cognitive-style/)). A high-level adaptor and a high-level innovator both produce excellent work, in different ways.

## Adaptor and innovator descriptors

Kirton's classic characterizations ([Regent review](https://www.regent.edu/journal/emerging-leadership-journeys/kirton-adaption-innovation/)):

Adaptors tend to solve problems rather than find them, seek solutions in tried and understood ways, produce a sufficiency of ideas closely based on existing definitions of the problem, maintain high accuracy over long detailed work, rarely challenge rules, preserve group cohesion, and provide a stable base for the innovator's riskier moves.

Innovators tend to find and redefine problems, approach tasks from unexpected angles, produce many ideas (some impractical and often beyond the agreed paradigm), treat rules and past custom as dispensable, and stay less attached to consensus or group cohesion, which adaptors can experience as abrasive or risky.

Neither style is right. Organizations need adaptors to keep systems reliable and innovators to break out of dead ends, and the friction between them is both the cost and the engine of the pair.

## The KAI inventory and its factors

The Kirton Adaption-Innovation Inventory (KAI) is a 32-item self-report measure that places a person on the continuum. Scores run from 32 to 160 with a theoretical midpoint of 96, and in general-population samples the mean is about 95 with a standard deviation near 16, so most people fall between roughly 79 and 113. Higher scores are more innovative and lower scores more adaptive, and the inventory's internal reliability (Cronbach's alpha) runs about 0.84 to 0.89 ([KAI Foundation](https://kai.foundation/adaption-innovation-measure-cognitive-style/); [Bobic et al. 1999](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/249671196_The_Kirton_Adaption-Innovation_Inventory)). It resolves into three factors:

1. Sufficiency of Originality (SO): a preference for producing many novel ideas versus a few sound, relevant ones.
2. Efficiency (E): a preference for thoroughness, precision, and methodical reliability.
3. Rule/Group Conformity (R/G): a preference for working within rules and group norms versus challenging them.

> [!note] Factor-structure debate
> The three-factor model is the original and standard one, but later work argues that Sufficiency of Originality splits into Idea Generation and Preference for Change, giving a better-fitting four-factor structure in some samples ([Im & Hu 2005](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2466/pr0.96.2.408-410)). Treat the three-factor model as the default, keeping in mind that the dimensionality is contested.

## Why it's used

A-I is applied in management, team design, and change leadership. Seeing that resistance to a new initiative is often a style difference, an adaptor defending a working paradigm rather than incompetence or obstruction, improves collaboration and helps compose teams that span the continuum ([KAI Foundation: why initiatives get blocked](https://kai.foundation/1131-2/)).
