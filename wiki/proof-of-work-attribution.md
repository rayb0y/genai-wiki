# Proof of Work: attributing contribution in a team

The Proof of Work agent in [[innovation-team-agent-architecture]] is the record of what the team built. The harder question behind it is who built what, and who is responsible for which idea. In a school or innovation project every artifact tends to carry the whole team's name, which hides the distribution of effort and defeats the point of a "what was built" record. This page is the research on whether contribution can actually be attributed from session transcripts and artifacts, what can be attributed and what cannot, and how to do it without turning the system into surveillance. The short version: participation and proposal-level attribution are feasible and useful, finished-artifact and single-idea ownership are mostly not, and the right output is a provisional map the team confirms, not a score the system assigns.

## Why attribution matters: identifiability and social loafing

The reason to attribute contribution at all is not bookkeeping, it is effort. Ringelmann's 1913 rope-pulling experiments found that as a group grows, each person pulls less hard, with eight-person teams producing well under the sum of their individual capacities. Latané, Williams and Harkins named this social loafing and showed it is motivational rather than a coordination artifact, using clapping and shouting tasks where coordination could not explain the drop ([Latané, Williams & Harkins 1979](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.37.6.822)). The same work isolated the antidote: when individual contributions became identifiable, effort recovered. Identifiability of contribution is therefore the lever, and it is also the principle behind individual accountability in cooperative learning, where group work only improves individual outcomes if each person's part is visible and assessed. Interviewee 3 reached the same conclusion from experience: a member who contributes nothing "becomes invisible" and is left with an "empty claim" on the project ([[interviewee-3-analysis]]). So a contribution record is worth building because visibility reduces free-riding, not because the team needs a leaderboard.

## What "responsible for what" can mean: contribution types, not single authors

The instinct to find the one person responsible for an artifact is usually the wrong frame. Scholarly publishing hit this problem decades ago and answered it with a vocabulary of contribution types rather than a single author. The Contributor Roles Taxonomy (CRediT) defines fourteen distinct roles, such as conceptualization, methodology, investigation, writing the original draft, and review and editing, and lets each be marked as lead, equal, or supporting ([Brand et al. 2015](https://doi.org/10.1002/leap.1210); [NISO CRediT](https://credit.niso.org/)). Studies using CRediT on real papers show labor is genuinely divided, with different people owning conceptual, analytical, and writing work ([Investigating the Division of Scientific Labor using CRediT, Quantitative Science Studies 2021](https://direct.mit.edu/qss/article/2/1/111/97558)). The lesson for the Proof of Work record is that "who is responsible" should resolve to a small set of contribution roles attached to an artifact, not a single name, and that the same person rarely owns the idea, the build, and the write-up.

This also means mapping the team to roles is reasonable, because not everyone is involved in making every artifact. Belbin's team-role model describes nine roles across thinking, social, and action categories (for example Plant for idea generation, Implementer for execution, Completer Finisher for quality), and argues that the mix, not any single role, drives outcomes ([Belbin team roles](https://www.belbin.com/about/belbin-team-roles)). Roles can be inferred from interaction and peer feedback rather than self-report alone, and work combining Belbin roles with social network analysis has been used to model who occupies which role from how people actually interact ([Towards Team Formation Using Belbin Role Types and a Social Networks Analysis Approach 2018](https://bura.brunel.ac.uk/bitstream/2438/18442/1/FullText.pdf)). A role map sets expectations: it tells the system that the idea-generator and the artifact-builder may be different people, so an artifact's author is not automatically the source of its idea.

## From transcripts: participation and proposal-level attribution

Transcripts support two kinds of attribution at different confidence levels.

The first is participation, which is reliable. Who spoke, in what order, and how much is a structural fact recoverable from a speaker-labeled transcript, and it is what the deterministic logger records (the "turns" in the ingestion layer). This is exactly the identifiability signal that fights social loafing, and it connects to the collective-intelligence finding that balanced turn-taking predicts group performance ([[team-dynamics]]).

The second is proposal and decision attribution, which is feasible but lower-confidence. Detecting decisions and action items from meeting transcripts is an established task with annotated corpora, including the AMI meeting corpus that labels decisions and actions, and models that classify utterances into decision, action, or discussion and tie them to a speaker ([Meeting Action Item Detection 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.16763)). This can answer "who first articulated this direction" or "who committed to this task." The limit is that a clean first-utterance is not the same as authorship of the idea, because in discussion an idea is usually reframed by several people before it settles, which is the distributed-cognition problem below. Proposal attribution is therefore best recorded as provenance ("this direction first appears in session three, proposed by X, then refined by the team") rather than as ownership.

## From artifacts: edit provenance, and the collective-name problem

Artifacts can be attributed only to the extent they carry an edit history. When work happens in a system that records edits, such as Google Docs revision history or version control, who wrote and changed what is recoverable. DocuViz visualizes the full revision history of a shared document precisely so that collaborators, and instructors, can see who contributed what rather than seeing only the finished file ([Wang et al. 2015](https://doi.org/10.1145/2702123.2702517)). Collaborative writing itself has a taxonomy of roles and strategies, so "contributed to the document" already decomposes into writing, reviewing, and structuring ([Lowry, Curtis & Lowry 2004](https://doi.org/10.1177/0021943603259363)).

The caution is that edit-based attribution credits the last editor, not the originator. In version control, a line is attributed to whoever last touched it, so reformatting, refactors, and file moves reassign authorship to people who reorganized rather than wrote the content, which is why authorship-attribution research exists beyond simple blame ([Dauber et al. 2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.05681)). The deeper problem is the one the user named: a finished artifact with everyone's name on it has usually lost the provenance entirely, because the artifact is the output of the team's process, not of one author. Edit provenance works going forward, when the team works in tools that log edits; it cannot be reconstructed from a flat final file.

## The hard limit: ideas are distributed, so "the source of an idea" is often a category error

The most important finding is also the most deflating for the idea-attribution goal. Distributed cognition treats the unit of cognition as a system of people and artifacts in a work practice, not an individual mind, and argues that ideas and knowledge emerge from the interactions between people and their tools rather than being owned by one head ([Hutchins 1995](https://arl.human.cornell.edu/linked%20docs/Hutchins_Distributed_Cognition.pdf); [Hollan, Hutchins & Kirsh 2000](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/353485.353487)). Interviewee 3 described this directly: the team's invisible transportation idea was rejected as blue-sky, then re-understood through discussion until it became proposable, with no single author of the final form. When a good idea is the product of stress-testing and reframing across several people, asking which one person is its source is often the wrong question. The defensible move is to record the genealogy of an idea (where it first appears, who moved it, how it changed) rather than to award it to an owner. This also fits the architecture's rule that the system never issues a verdict, and Interviewee 3's insistence that AI give pros and cons and provenance, not a judgment of who is right or who owns what.

## Sensitivity: attribution must not become surveillance

Contribution tracking is sensitive in a way the rest of the system is not, because it is one step from monitoring and ranking people. The same identifiability that reduces social loafing can, if it becomes a covert score, damage the psychological safety that lets teams function ([[team-dynamics]]), and Interviewee 3 warned that handing the accountability conversation to an AI can erode the ownership and team spirit that make a team a team ([[interviewee-3-analysis]]). His constructive alternative is the design constraint here: rather than the system judging contribution, it should surface the structure of what happened (in his framing, the points of agreement and disagreement across members) so the people can have the accountable conversation themselves. So attribution should be visible to the team and confirmed by them, not computed about them and held privately, and the Collaboration channel's confidentiality rules apply.

## Implications for the Proof of Work agent

The research points to a narrower and more honest role than "figure out who did what." The Proof of Work agent should:

Record participation from the transcript as a reliable, visible signal, because identifiability is the lever against social loafing, while stopping short of ranking people.

Attribute at the proposal and edit level, not the artifact level: who first articulated a direction (from transcript decision detection) and who edited what (from artifact revision history where it exists), each carried as provenance with a confidence level, not as ownership.

Use a contribution-type vocabulary rather than a single author, on the CRediT model, and a role map (Belbin or emergent) so the system does not assume the artifact's editor is the idea's source.

Record idea genealogy rather than idea ownership, because distributed cognition makes single-source attribution unreliable for collaboratively evolved ideas.

Propose, never decide: present the contribution and provenance map to the team for confirmation or correction, consistent with the architecture's "the system never issues a verdict" rule and Interviewee 3's no-verdicts requirement, so the record stays a tool the team owns rather than a score applied to them.

What it should not attempt is reconstructing authorship from a finished, jointly-named artifact with no edit trail, or assigning a single owner to an idea that several people shaped. Those are the cases the research says are not recoverable, and pretending otherwise would manufacture false precision about people, which is the most damaging error this part of the system could make.

## A role-and-contribution map, done right

The useful, defensible version of "map the team" separates two things that a single dashboard ring tends to blur: role and contribution. They answer different questions and rest on different evidence.

A contribution is a specific thing a person did, tagged by type and pinned to a receipt. This is the CRediT model: rather than one author per artifact, attach a small vocabulary of contribution types (conceptualization, research, design, engineering, writing, facilitation, review) to concrete decisions and artifacts, each marked lead, equal, or supporting ([Brand et al. 2015](https://doi.org/10.1002/leap.1210)). A contribution entry reads "led the design of the wireframe (artifact 12), supporting on the research synthesis (doc 4)." Contributions are verbs with evidence behind them.

A role is the stable pattern that emerges from those contributions over time. This is the Belbin layer: someone who keeps seeding directions is functioning as the idea-generator, someone who keeps turning concepts into built things is the implementer, someone who keeps drawing quiet members in and cooling heated moments is the coordinator-facilitator ([Belbin team roles](https://www.belbin.com/about/belbin-team-roles)). The research detail that matters is that roles should be inferred from interaction and then confirmed, not taken from a self-report questionnaire, because how people actually behave is a more honest signal than how they describe themselves ([Towards Team Formation Using Belbin Role Types and Social Network Analysis 2018](https://bura.brunel.ac.uk/bitstream/2438/18442/1/FullText.pdf)). Role is the summary noun; contributions are what justify it.

Each input carries its own reliability, and the map should show it rather than hide it behind equal-looking slices. Participation (meeting turns) is reliable. Artifact contribution is reliable only where an edit history exists ([Wang et al. 2015](https://doi.org/10.1145/2702123.2702517)). Proposals and decisions come from transcript decision-detection at moderate confidence ([Meeting Action Item Detection 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.16763)). Facilitation is a soft inference, flagged as lower-confidence. Idea-origin is never rendered as a number or a slice, because distributed cognition makes single-source ownership a category error ([Hutchins 1995](https://arl.human.cornell.edu/linked%20docs/Hutchins_Distributed_Cognition.pdf)); it is recorded as a genealogy line instead, "this direction first appears from X in session 3, then the team reshaped it."

As an artifact, the per-member record is four plain parts and deliberately no ring, no percentages, and no "time spent." A confirmed role line ("Primary: Researcher. Secondary: Facilitator") the member can correct. An evidence-linked contribution log, each row tagged with type, the artifact or decision it points to, lead or equal or supporting, and a confidence level. An idea genealogy, the directions this person seeded or moved, written as provenance with the session each first appeared. And a short narrative summary in the member's own words.

Two properties keep it a tool rather than a scoreboard. It is member-owned and confirmable: the system proposes, the person edits, nothing is a covert verdict, which satisfies both the architecture's no-verdict rule and Interviewee 3's objection ([[interviewee-3-analysis]]). And it is evidence-linked: every role claim traces to specific contributions and every contribution to a specific artifact or decision, so it is auditable rather than a vibe, and hard to game because the receipts must exist (a defense against the Goodhart problem that any visible metric invites).

There is also a team-level view that is not surveillance, because it is about coverage, not ranking. A role-coverage map asks whether the team has the functions it needs and flags an obvious gap, pointing the team at a structural hole rather than at a person. The line not to cross is lining the individual cards up side by side to compare people, because identical-looking cards invite ranking, and ranking is the version that damages psychological safety ([[team-dynamics]]). Same data, opposite ethics, decided by whether the record faces the contributor (for recognition and self-advocacy, which fixes Interviewee 3's "empty claim") or faces a manager (for comparison).

## How well is role coverage actually supported?

The coverage idea has to be held honestly, because the evidence is weaker than the popular Belbin framing implies, and the page should not oversell it.

Two cautions come straight from the literature. First, the Belbin Team-Role Self-Perception Inventory, the usual source of the nine roles, has weak psychometric properties: an independent assessment found low internal-consistency coefficients and a factor structure that did not match the model, partly because its ipsative (forced-choice) scoring is hard to analyze ([Furnham, Steele & Pendleton 1993](https://bpspsychub.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.2044-8325.1993.tb00535.x)), and later work argued the roles may largely reduce to the Big Five personality factors. Belbin's own reply was that it is a tool for management practice, not a psychometric instrument, which is fair but also means it should not be treated as a measurement. A balanced review of the model's development and validity is [Aritzeta, Swailes & Senior 2007](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-6486.2007.00666.x). Second, the specific claim that role-balanced teams outperform unbalanced ones is mixed, not settled: some studies support it, notably that mixed-role teams beat homogeneous teams made of one role type ([Prichard & Stanton 1999](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Testing-Belbin's-Team-Role-Theory-of-Effective-Prichard-Stanton/8746dd6a6c6861a4a7b84802c41608db37157749)), while others find no significant relationship between balance and performance ([Partington & Harris 1999](https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/02621719910293783/full/html)).

So coverage should be a heuristic prompt, not a validated score, and it is more defensible at a coarse grain than as nine fine-grained types. The older and sturdier distinction is functional: Benne and Sheats classified group roles into task roles (moving the work forward) and group-building or maintenance roles (keeping the group working as a group), plus self-oriented roles ([Benne & Sheats 1948](https://spssi.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1540-4560.1948.tb01783.x)). A team that has task functions but no maintenance function, or the reverse, is the kind of gap worth surfacing, and it does not depend on the contested Belbin taxonomy.

The better-evidenced construct, though, is not which role types are present but whether each member's role is clear and accepted. Role ambiguity, the lack of clear information about one's scope, behaviors, and how one will be evaluated, is reliably linked to lower role efficacy, weaker cohesion, lower satisfaction, and poorer role performance ([Beauchamp et al. 2002](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232482058_Role_ambiguity_role_efficacy_and_role_performance_Multidimensional_and_mediational_relationships_within_interdependent_sport_teams); [Bray & Brawley 2002](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/104649640203300204)). That points the coverage view at its highest-value job: less "does the team have the right mix of types," which is weakly supported, and more "does each person have a clear, accepted role," which is well supported. In practice the team-level map should flag a coarse functional gap (no one doing maintenance, no one closing tasks) and surface role clarity (does each member know their scope and how their part is judged), and present both as prompts for the team rather than as a balance metric the system has validated.
